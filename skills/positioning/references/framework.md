# Positioning framework (in depth)

This reference expands the six-component method in `SKILL.md`. It is adapted from April Dunford's positioning work (*Obviously Awesome*), which is the most reliable framework for technical products because it derives positioning from context (alternatives + a chosen category) rather than from clever slogans.

Read this when a positioning problem is knotty, when the user wants the *why*, or when the quick pass in `SKILL.md` produced something that still feels generic.

## The central idea

Positioning is **context-setting**. The same product can look boring or magical depending on what the customer compares it to. So positioning is not "what words do we use" — it's "what should the customer believe this is, and measure it against." Get the frame right and the value becomes self-evident; get it wrong and no amount of polished copy rescues it.

That is why the method starts with *alternatives* and *category*, not with adjectives.

## Working each component

### 1. Competitive alternatives

Ask: **"If this product disappeared tomorrow, what would the customer do instead?"**

- The honest answer is often a spreadsheet, a manual workflow, a junior employee's time, or "live with the problem." Name the real one, not the aspirational competitor.
- The alternative sets the bar for value. "10x faster" only means something against a specific baseline.

### 2. Unique attributes

Ask: **"What do we have that the alternative does not?"**

- List capabilities and characteristics that are *factually true and verifiable* — not opinions ("easy to use") but facts ("imports directly from QuickBooks", "runs on-device, no data leaves the network").
- If an attribute is also true of the alternative, it is not differentiating — drop it.

### 3. Value (the feature → benefit bridge)

For each unique attribute, ask: **"...which means the customer can ___ / no longer has to ___."**

- This is the step builders skip. A feature is what the product *does*; value is what the customer *gets*. Keep asking "so what?" until you reach an outcome the customer actually cares about.
- Example chain: *runs on-device* → *no caller audio leaves the agency network* → *the agency can adopt it without a new data-sharing review* → *legal says yes in weeks, not quarters*.

### 4. Best-fit customers

Ask: **"Who has this problem so badly that the value is obvious and urgent?"**

- Define the segment by the *characteristics that make them care*, not by demographics. "Teams that reconcile invoices across 3+ systems monthly" beats "finance teams."
- Narrowing feels like leaving money on the table. It does the opposite: a sharp segment makes the value undeniable to the people most likely to buy, and they tell others.

### 5. Market category

Ask: **"What should the customer think this *is*, so the value is immediately obvious?"**

- The category is the mental shelf you want to sit on. It tells the customer what to compare you to and which of your attributes matter.
- Choose a category where your unique attributes are strengths. Avoid categories crowded with giants unless you can win a sub-segment of it.

### 6. Relevant trend (optional)

Ask: **"Is there a real shift that makes this matter *now*?"**

- A trend adds urgency ("everyone's moving to X"). Use it only if true and relevant; a forced trend reads as hype and erodes trust. When in doubt, leave it out.

## Synthesizing the output

**Internal positioning statement** (for alignment, not for the website):

> For **[best-fit customer]** who **[situation/need]**, **[product]** is a **[category]** that **[primary unique value]**, unlike **[alternative]**, because **[key unique attribute]**.

**One-liner** (external): the positioning statement, compressed to plain language and led by the outcome. Aim for ~15 words or fewer. No buzzwords. It should be understandable by someone who has never seen the product.

**Value proposition** (external): 2–4 sentences expanding the one-liner — who it's for, the problem, what it does, and the payoff.

## Common traps

- **The "for everyone" trap.** Trying to appeal to all customers makes the value vague for each. Pick the sharpest segment.
- **Feature-listing.** A list of capabilities is not positioning. Capabilities only matter once the customer knows what to compare them against and why.
- **Comparing to the wrong alternative.** Positioning against a famous competitor when customers actually use a spreadsheet makes the message miss.
- **Hedged category.** "An AI-powered platform for X, Y, and Z" hides the real frame. One clear category beats three vague ones.
- **Buzzwords as filler.** "Seamless, robust, next-generation" are words used when the writer hasn't decided what the product actually is. Replace each with a specific claim.
