# Swipe file: weak vs. strong positioning (developer/technical products)

Calibration examples. Each shows a typical "before" (how a builder first describes the product) and a sharper "after," with notes on what changed. Use these to judge whether a draft is actually good — not to copy the wording.

These examples are illustrative (not real companies' copy).

---

## 1. A CI/CD tool

**Before:** "A powerful, flexible automation platform with a robust pipeline engine, YAML configuration, and seamless integrations."

**After:** "Ship code without babysitting your build. For small teams on GitHub, [Tool] runs your tests and deploys on every push — set up in one file, no dedicated build engineer required."

**What changed:**
- Led with the outcome ("ship without babysitting") instead of the engine.
- Named a best-fit segment (small teams on GitHub) instead of "everyone."
- Replaced buzzwords (powerful, flexible, robust, seamless) with specific claims (one file, no build engineer).
- Implied the real alternative: hand-rolled scripts + someone maintaining them.

---

## 2. A database / data product

**Before:** "A next-generation, highly scalable distributed datastore with tunable consistency and a rich query layer."

**After:** "A database for apps that outgrew Postgres but aren't ready to hire a DBA. Handles billions of rows with millisecond reads, and scales by adding nodes — no re-architecture."

**What changed:**
- Anchored on the real alternative and the moment of pain ("outgrew Postgres, no DBA").
- Turned attributes (scalable, distributed) into outcomes (billions of rows, ms reads, no re-architecture).
- Picked a clear category frame (a database for a specific growth stage).

---

## 3. An observability / monitoring tool

**Before:** "Unified, end-to-end observability with logs, metrics, and traces in a single pane of glass, powered by AI."

**After:** "Find out why production broke in minutes, not hours. For on-call engineers, [Tool] connects a user's error to the exact log line and deploy that caused it."

**What changed:**
- Replaced the category cliché ("single pane of glass") with the job to be done (find the cause fast).
- Targeted the person who feels the pain at 3 a.m. (on-call engineers).
- Made the value concrete (error → exact log line + deploy) instead of listing telemetry types.
- Dropped "AI-powered" — it was a mechanism, not a benefit.

---

## 4. An API / developer service

**Before:** "A comprehensive API platform offering a powerful suite of endpoints for managing your data with enterprise-grade reliability."

**After:** "Add address autocomplete to your checkout in an afternoon. One REST endpoint, 99.99% uptime, and pricing that starts free."

**What changed:**
- Led with a concrete thing the developer can ship today, and how fast.
- Quantified the reliability claim instead of saying "enterprise-grade."
- Removed "comprehensive / powerful suite" — scope words that say nothing.

---

## Patterns across the good examples

- **Every "after" opens with an outcome or a job**, not a description of the system.
- **Every "after" names or strongly implies a specific user** and the alternative they're escaping.
- **Numbers and concrete nouns replace adjectives.** "Billions of rows," "one file," "exact log line."
- **Buzzwords are gone.** If a phrase could sit on any competitor's homepage, it was cut.
